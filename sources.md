# Content Sources — sites.json (v1: Otrar, Sygnak, Ulytau, Saraishyk, Sarai-Batu)

Keep this file updated every time you add or change a fact. It protects you if a judge
asks "how do you know that," and doubles as a works-cited reference.

- **Otrar**: Wikipedia "Otrar" and "Otrar Catastrophe"; Encyclopaedia Iranica ("Otrar");
  UNESCO Central Asian regional report on Otrar. Siege dates (Dec 1219 - Feb 1220) and
  Timur's death there (Jan 1405) corroborated across multiple sources.
- **Sygnak**: Grokipedia "Sighnaq"; e-history.kz articles on Sygnak's role as White Horde
  capital and its 1219 siege by Jochi; welcome.kz tourism page (mint established ~1328,
  excavation history since 1948).
- **Ulytau**: e-history.kz / altynorda.kazakhstan.travel articles on the Golden Horde's
  750th anniversary; Wikipedia "Golden Horde" (Timur's 1391 campaign passing through
  Ulytau, epitaph reference). Note: the 1269 Talas kurultai (division of the Mongol
  Empire into uluses) took place in the Talas valley near the modern Kazakhstan-
  Kyrgyzstan border, NOT at Ulytau - do not conflate the two in future content.
- **Saraishyk**: The Astana Times ("Uncovering Kazakhstan's Volga-Caspian Gateways to
  Silk Roads," 2025); Grokipedia "Saray-Jük"; Wikipedia "Saray-Jük"; orexca.com city
  history page. Destruction date (1580) and Ibn Battuta's 1334 visit confirmed across
  sources.
- **Sarai / New Sarai**: Wikipedia "Sarai (city)"; Britannica "Golden Horde"; Kiddle
  "Sarai (city) facts for kids." Founding tied to William of Rubruck's 1253-54 account;
  final destruction in 1502 by Meñli I Giray of Crimea confirmed by multiple sources as
  the symbolic end of the Golden Horde.

## Known open questions / things to double check before presenting as fact

- Exact founding date of Otrar as a settlement is disputed (2nd century BCE Kangju-era
  vs. later); for the timeline's purposes it's treated as "pre-1206" since it predates
  the Golden Horde entirely.
- Scholars disagree on whether "Old Sarai" and "New Sarai" were the same site at
  different times or two genuinely separate archaeological locations - flagged as an
  open historiographical debate, worth mentioning in the app's "About the data" note
  rather than presenting one theory as settled fact.
- Coordinates for all five sites are approximate (city/oasis-level precision), based on
  known modern reference points (e.g. Otrar near Turkestan, Sarai near Selitrennoye,
  Russia) - refine with GPS data from museum/archaeological sources if precision matters
  for your final map.

## Recommended next sources to consult as you expand

- Vadim Trepavlov, István Vásáry, Charles Halperin, Uli Schamiloglu - academic
  historians specializing in the Golden Horde; search Google Scholar for their papers.
- National Museum of Kazakhstan and the Ulytau State Historical-Cultural
  Museum-Reserve - for locally authoritative facts and a credibility boost with judges.
- historical-basemaps GitHub repo (aourednik/historical-basemaps) - for border
  GeoJSON snapshots to pair with this site data on the map itself.

# v2 additions — 9 new cities, 9 battle sites, 6 new routes

- **Bolgar**: Wikipedia "Golden Horde"; academia.edu paper "Plan of the Golden Horde
  Bolgar town." Confirmed as an early Mongol conquest (1236) that became an important
  early minting city.
- **Azaq (Tana)**: Wikipedia "Golden Horde" (Mengu-Timur's 1267 grant of Genoese/
  Venetian trading rights); academia.edu "Structure of the Golden Horde cities at the
  time of khan Uzbek"; Grokipedia "Timeline of the Golden Horde" (1395 sack by Timur,
  sparing Muslim inhabitants).
- **Solkhat**: Wikipedia "Golden Horde" and Grokipedia "Timeline of the Golden Horde"
  (Ozbeg's 1314 mosque, Islamization); academia.edu paper on Crimean Golden Horde
  cities (Solkhat vs. later Crimean Khanate cities).
- **Hajji Tarkhan**: Kings and Generals podcast transcript ("Ages of Conquest" episode
  8 on the Golden Horde, citing Ibn Battuta); Grokipedia "Timeline of the Golden Horde"
  for the 1395 destruction by Timur.
- **Ukek**: ResearchGate "Where Was Sarai" and "The Rise of Urban Centers in the
  Golden Horde and the City of Ukek" - both academic papers specifically on Ukek's
  location and role; 1395 destruction confirmed via Grokipedia timeline.
- **Majar**: Kings and Generals podcast transcript, directly quoting Ibn Battuta's
  list of Khan Ozbeg's great cities ("al-Kafa, al-Kirim, al-Machar, Azaq, Sudaq and
  Khwarezm").
- **Yasi (Turkestan)**: general knowledge of the Yasawi mausoleum and Timur's 1389
  construction order - this is widely documented but worth a dedicated verification
  pass (e.g. UNESCO's listing for the Yasawi Mausoleum) before final publication,
  especially the Taj Mahal architectural-influence claim, which is a commonly repeated
  but not universally precise comparison.
- **Sauran**: general historical knowledge of Syr Darya fortress cities; worth a
  dedicated source check (e.g. UNESCO tentative list documentation for the
  Fergana-Syrdarya Corridor, mentioned in the Saraishyk search results above) before
  presenting exact dates as certain.
- **Jand**: general historical knowledge of early Syr Darya Islamic cities and the
  1219-1220 Mongol campaign; less precisely dated in sources consulted so far - flag
  for a follow-up academic source before treating specifics as settled.
- **Battle sites (Kalka, Kyiv, Legnica, Mohi, Blue Waters, Vozha, Kulikovo, Kondurcha,
  Terek)**: Wikipedia "Golden Horde"; Grokipedia "Timeline of the Golden Horde"
  (Kondurcha and Terek dates and details, Timur's 1395 destruction campaign
  specifically naming Ukek, Azaq, Hajji Tarkhan, and Sarai Berke); general historical
  consensus on Kalka (1223), Kyiv (Dec 1240), Legnica/Mohi (1241), Blue Waters
  (c. 1362), Vozha (1378), and Kulikovo (1380) dates - all well-documented,
  low-controversy dates in mainstream historiography.
- **Routes**: constructed by connecting the site data above along historically
  plausible corridors (Volga river route, Crimean-Genoese trade axis via Mengu-Timur's
  1267 grant, Khwarezm route, and the military campaign paths of Batu, Mamai, Timur,
  Edigu, and Algirdas). Route paths themselves are illustrative connections between
  real, sourced endpoints rather than precise historical trade-route surveys - fine
  for a prototype, but flag as approximate in the app's "About the data" note.

## Open items to verify before final presentation

- Yasi/Turkestan's Taj Mahal architectural-influence claim - soften or drop if you
  can't find a citation you're comfortable defending live.
- Sauran and Jand's specific founding/decline dates are looser than the rest of the
  dataset - treat as approximate until cross-checked against a dedicated source.
- "Destroyed: null" fields (Solkhat, Yasi) mean the site persisted past 1502 and is
  still inhabited today - make sure your timeline-filtering code treats null as "still
  standing," not as missing data.
